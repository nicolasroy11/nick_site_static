white privilege

No matter the intention, poor PR kills the best—laid argument

My name is Nicolas Roy. I was born in Montreal, and lived there until the age of 13. I then moved to Los Angeles and have lived here ever since. I am fluent in French and English. I am also sufficiently fluent in Spanish to make your average Angeleno think that I am fluent in Spanish. I have a bachelor's degree in electrical engineering from the University of Southern California, as well as some ongoing graduate coursework at the same institution. I graduated in December 2010.

My degree was focused specifically in hardware, and the first couple of jobs I had reflected my interest in it. As the years went by, however, I began to gravitate towards jobs that involved programming in any capacity, whether scripting VB6 for die-testing machines or making C firmware for 8051 chips controlling switching devices and touch screen UX.

My last job title was 'programmer' for CMLRW Security. This is a company out of Denver that specialized in automation for high security places, mostly jails. My job there consisted in programming HMIs (Human Machine interface) for jail staffers. I also programmed PLC hardware that communicated with the HMIs. The HMI part was done in Indusoft using VBScript, but 80% of the HMI/PLC tasks were repetitive enough that I just wrote Excel VBA script to automate them. My automation templates were popular with management and are still in use at the office.

The original arrangement with this company was that I would work remotely, and I did for almost 2 years. The freedom of working remotely meant that I could travel without having to take vacation time. In fact, for the duration of my employment at CMLRW, I did not make use of any of it. My fianc√©e and I visited 7 countries in all. All the while, I am proud to say, I did not miss a beat in my projects, and was never late to a single online meeting!

The flip side of working for CMLRW is that it afforded me an intimate look at the goings on in your typical American jail setting. I grew rather close to some of the guards and became privy to the more sordid bits of information regarding prisoners as well as other staffers at each jail.

Some tales have the sobering ability to jar you back into reality if you had any faith in human nature. These tales had that in spades. Although I would do well to avoid going into detail about most of what I heard, I can say that being a prison guard is not for everyone. I can also say that I would never marry a female guard working at a jail, given how common it is for intimacy to develop between female guards and male inmates, with absolutely disastrous results for everyone involved (think deadly venereal diseases for all).

I left CMLRW when new management decided that remote work was no longer tolerable. I was asked to move to Denver, but refused and was discharged as a result.
